# Domain 2: Business Continuity, Disaster Recovery, and Incident Response Concepts

## How to Use This Domain

Use this domain to understand how organizations prepare for disruption, recover from disasters, and respond to security incidents. The exam expects you to know the purpose and main components of business continuity, disaster recovery, and incident response.

## Section 1: Business Continuity

### What You Should Understand

Business continuity keeps essential operations running during disruption. The focus is business survival and maintaining critical services, even if normal operations are affected.

### Key Concepts

- Business continuity planning identifies critical functions and how to keep them operating.
- A business impact analysis identifies important processes, dependencies, and impact of downtime.
- Continuity plans should define people, processes, communication, alternate resources, and recovery priorities.
- Plans must be tested and updated.

### Important Terms

- Business continuity - Ability to continue critical operations during disruption.
- BCP - Business Continuity Plan.
- BIA - Business Impact Analysis.
- Critical function - Business activity that must continue or recover quickly.
- RTO - Recovery Time Objective; target time to restore service.
- RPO - Recovery Point Objective; acceptable amount of data loss measured in time.
- Continuity strategy - Planned method to maintain essential services.

### What To Practice

- Identify critical services for a small company.
- Decide possible RTO and RPO values for email, payroll, and customer support.
- Write a short continuity checklist for a power outage.

### Quick Check

1. What is the purpose of business continuity?
2. What does a BIA identify?
3. What is RTO?
4. What is RPO?
5. Why should continuity plans be tested?

## Section 2: Disaster Recovery

### What You Should Understand

Disaster recovery focuses on restoring technology systems and data after a major disruption. It is more technical than business continuity and often includes backups, alternate sites, restoration steps, and recovery testing.

### Key Concepts

- Disaster recovery supports business continuity.
- Backups must be protected, tested, and available when needed.
- Recovery plans should define recovery order and responsibilities.
- Alternate processing sites may be hot, warm, or cold.
- Recovery testing confirms that systems and data can actually be restored.

### Important Terms

- Disaster recovery - Restoring IT systems after a disruption.
- DRP - Disaster Recovery Plan.
- Backup - Copy of data used for restoration.
- Restore - Returning data or systems from backup.
- Hot site - Fully ready alternate site.
- Warm site - Partially ready alternate site.
- Cold site - Basic facility requiring setup before use.
- Failover - Switching to backup systems or sites.

### What To Practice

- Compare hot, warm, and cold sites.
- Explain why untested backups are risky.
- Create a recovery order for domain controller, file server, email, and public website.

### Quick Check

1. What is the purpose of disaster recovery?
2. How does DR support business continuity?
3. What is the difference between hot, warm, and cold sites?
4. Why must backups be tested?
5. What is failover?

## Section 3: Incident Response

### What You Should Understand

Incident response is the structured process of handling security incidents. It helps reduce damage, preserve evidence, restore systems, and improve defenses after the incident.

### Key Concepts

- Events are observable activities.
- Alerts are notifications that need review.
- Incidents are confirmed or likely harmful security events.
- Incident response should follow a prepared plan or playbook.
- Documentation is required for timeline, evidence, decisions, and handoff.

### Incident Response Phases

- Preparation - Build tools, plans, playbooks, training, and contacts.
- Detection and analysis - Identify and understand suspicious activity.
- Containment - Limit spread and damage.
- Eradication - Remove the cause.
- Recovery - Restore normal operations safely.
- Lessons learned - Review what happened and improve controls.

### Important Terms

- Event - Recorded activity.
- Alert - Signal that something may need review.
- Incident - Security event that may harm systems, data, or operations.
- Triage - Initial review and prioritization.
- Containment - Limiting impact.
- Eradication - Removing root cause.
- Recovery - Returning to normal operation.
- Lessons learned - Review after incident resolution.

### What To Practice

- Classify examples as event, alert, or incident.
- Write a basic phishing incident response checklist.
- Create a timeline from sample incident facts.

### Quick Check

1. What is the difference between an alert and an incident?
2. Why is preparation part of incident response?
3. What does containment do?
4. What does eradication remove?
5. Why are lessons learned important?

## Section 4: BC, DR, and IR Comparison

### What You Should Understand

Business continuity, disaster recovery, and incident response are related but not the same.

### Key Concepts

- Business continuity focuses on keeping essential business functions operating.
- Disaster recovery focuses on restoring technology and data.
- Incident response focuses on handling security incidents.
- All three require planning, ownership, communication, testing, and improvement.

### Quick Comparison

| Area | Main Focus | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Business Continuity | Keep critical operations running | Continue customer support during outage |
| Disaster Recovery | Restore IT systems and data | Restore servers from backup |
| Incident Response | Handle security incident | Contain malware infection |

### What To Practice

- For a ransomware scenario, identify the BC, DR, and IR activities.
- Explain which team might be involved in each area.
- Identify communication needs during a major outage.

### Quick Check

1. Which plan keeps business functions running?
2. Which plan restores systems and data?
3. Which plan handles security incidents?
4. Why do all three require testing?
5. Why is communication important during disruption?
